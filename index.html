Barbara McClintock

Pioneer geneticist and Nobel laureate

Barbara working in her laboratory.

Timeline of Barbara's life:

  • 1902 - Born in Hartford, Connecticut.
  • 1919 - Graduated high school and enrolled in Cornell University's College of Agriculture.
  • 1927 - Graduated with a Ph.D. in botany.
  • 1929 - Published a paper in Genetics on the charaterization of triploid maize chromosomes, which sparked further scientific interest in maize cytogenetics.
  • 1930 - Was the first person to describe the cross-like structure of homologous chromosomes during meiosis.
  • 1930 - Began working at Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory.
  • 1944 - Elected a member of the National Academy of Sciences.
  • 1944 - Became the first female president of the Genetics Society of America.
  • 1948 - Discovered the effects of the transposition of genetic loci Dissociator (Ds) and Activator (Ac).
  • 1957 - Started researchubg the evolutuon of indigenous strains of maize in Central America and South America.
  • 1967 - Officially retired, but continued working with graduate students and colleagues at the Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory.
  • 1983 - Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for "her discovery of mobile genetic elements".
  • 1992 - Died at the age of 90.
  • 2005 - Honored by the US Postal Service as part of an "American Scientists" commerative postage stamp series.
"I was just so interested in what I was doing I could hardly wait to get up in the morning and get at it. One of my friends, a geneticist, said I was a child, because only children can't wait to get up in the morning to get at what they want to do."
- Barbara McClintock
